## 1. Trapeze (Primary Incumbent)

### What Trapeze is
Full suite: PASS (scheduling/dispatching), ViewPoint (dispatch monitoring), DriverMate (driver app), PASS-MON, PASS-WEB/EZ Wallet, PASS-INFO, Trapeze EAM (maintenance), Ripple (notifications), MGM (on-board credit card). On-premises, commonly 10-25+ physical servers. Deep configurability for power users.

### Strengths to acknowledge
- **Control and configurability**: Power users love granular control — locking trips, freezing routes, adjusting batch parameters. Not trivial to replace.
- **Institutional knowledge**: Staff with 10-20 years of muscle memory. Every shortcut, every workaround. Real and valuable.
- **Industry default**: Safe, known choice for procurement teams and board members.

### Where Trapeze falls short
- **Antiquated UI**: Dozens of clicks for basic tasks. Months of training. Mapping severely outdated — renamed streets missing, staff cross-reference Google Maps.
- **On-prem burden**: 25+ servers (OCTA). Every upgrade is a project.
- **Manual-heavy**: Scheduling relies on human intervention. Paper DVIR. Maintenance/ops don't communicate digitally (Google Sheet down lists).
- **No modern rider experience**: No real rider app. Limited "Where's My Ride?" portal. Basic Twilio IVR bolted on.
- **No continuous optimization**: Batches at intervals (15 min). Schedule degrades through the day.
- **Fragmented product**: EAM doesn't talk to PASS. Eligibility separate. Notifications bolted on. MGM credit card hardware unreliable.
- **Talent drain**: Multiple ex-Trapeze hires at Spare. Market reads the signal.

### How to position
- Never trash directly — respect the people who use it.
- Ask questions that surface pain (address search, click count, maintenance-dispatch communication, new hire training time).
- Acknowledge transition difficulty honestly. Reference CapMetro (20 yrs on Trapeze, 95% OTP month 1) and OCTA (full PASS stack replacement).
- Highlight what Trapeze can't do: continuous optimization, modern rider app, AI Voice (44% of calls at PSTA), Open Fleets, single SaaS platform.

### Change management play
When staff can't imagine better: shadow operations, identify normalized pain, play it back specifically, offer demo videos, reference agencies who made the same transition.

## 2. RideCo

### Current position (early 2026)
Spare's most direct enterprise paratransit competitor. Aggressively winning large deals. Claims 5 of 10 largest US cities:
- **NYC MTA Access-A-Ride** (Oct 2025) — 1,100+ vehicles, largest paratransit in NA. Phased rollout 2026-2027.
- **MARTA Atlanta** (Dec 2025) — 200+ vehicle ADA fleet + MARTA Reach microtransit.
- **SEPTA Philadelphia** — 411 para vehicles, deployed in 4.5 months. Flagship reference.
- **Riverside Transit Agency** (Aug 2025) — ADA paratransit expansion.
- Also: Houston METRO, KCATA, Las Vegas RTC, San Antonio VIA, Albuquerque, Niagara.
- Launched **AI Agent** (call center automation) and **Flex Fleets** (TNC/taxi integration).
- Five US patents on continuous optimization. Deloitte Fast 50 three consecutive years.

### What they lead with
**"The Safe Choice for Large Paratransit"** — enterprise scale, big logos, patented continuous optimization (every 60 seconds), aggressive simulations with detailed KPI promises, speed to value (SEPTA in 4.5 months), strong support ratios, low TCO.

**Simulation-driven ROI**: Polished outputs promising 10-30% productivity gains, 90%+ OTP, millions in savings. At OCTA: claimed $10.3M/year.

**Pre-RFP political work**: At Milwaukee, embedded with disability advocacy groups months before RFP. Similar at Valley Metro. Show up early, socialize simulations, become the safe default.

**Price aggression**: At OCTA priced 25-32% below Spare over 7 years. Consistently undercuts on long-term deals.

### Where RideCo falls short

**Dispatcher control & algorithm rigidity** — LRCOG, Escambia, Gwinnett describe as over-automated. Dispatchers can't easily override, move riders, accommodate walk-ups. LRCOG: "lost all control."

**Eligibility** — Salesforce-based, not truly integrated with ops. Limited functionality, no automated follow-ups, agencies maintain separate systems alongside.

**Multimodal reality** — Despite "Time Snapping" marketing, confirmed to some agencies (Gwinnett) they don't support real multimodal. LA Metro: microtransit competed with fixed route rather than feeding it.

**Reporting** — Described as "trash" and non-self-serve by multiple customers. BI dashboards look flexible in demos but require RideCo for changes.

**Driver app & traffic** — No live traffic integration. Drivers switch to Google Maps/Waze. Own webinar cited ~85% paratransit OTP.

**Integrations** — Harris County: Uber API took ~2 years. LRCOG: no price visibility across TNC providers. Not an open ecosystem.

### Deal examples

**Losses to RideCo:**
- Milwaukee: Pre-positioned via advocacy groups. More impressive simulations. Spare chose conservative assumptions.
- Valley Metro: Both scored strong. RideCo won on "adaptability" — live rule changes, engine internals in demo. Spare demo too high-level.
- BFT, Greater Bridgeport, Round Rock, Sun Metro: RideCo positioned as modern DR default. Spare late to shape spec or underweighted on local relationships.

**Wins over RideCo:**
- OCTA: Spare selected over RideCo and Via. Won on multimodal, paratransit depth, migration credibility.
- LA Metro: Replaced RideCo microtransit. Under Spare: >90% app bookings, ~2.5 PPVH, ~2,000 daily boardings by day 4.
- Airdrie (AB): Active RideCo→Spare switch. Abandoned wheelchair trips, poor support, no integrated eligibility.

### How to differentiate

1. **Re-frame "safe choice"**: Safety isn't just big logos — it's control, eligibility integrity, analytics. Use LA Metro, Airdrie, LRCOG pain.
2. **Dispatcher control**: Show live overrides, reassignments, walk-up accommodation. Contrast "system knows best."
3. **Multimodal & Open Fleets**: LA Metro + OCTA + PSTA. Spare orchestrates fixed + micro + para + NDSP in production.
4. **Eligibility & analytics as first-class**: Demo real flows. Salesforce-based eligibility is brittle vs. native.
5. **Counter negative campaigning**: Expect "Spare can't do pre-scheduled ADA, optimization broken, lost LA Metro." Prepare with CapMetro, PSTA, LA Metro actuals.
6. **Simulation honesty**: With experienced operators (MV, Transdev), honest numbers win. "We lost Milwaukee because we refused to inflate. Here's what actually happened at CapMetro and PSTA."

## 3. Via

### Current position (early 2026)
IPO'd Sept 2025 on NYSE at $46/share (~$3.5B valuation), raised ~$493M. Revenue $434M in 2025 (31% YoY). Still unprofitable (losses narrowing). 689 customers, 30+ countries. 90%+ revenue from government. Acquired Remix (planning), Citymapper (trip planning), Downtowner (post-IPO). Launched "Via AI Labs." CEO eyeing more acquisitions.

### What they lead with
**"Turnkey, global microtransit leader"** — software + drivers + vehicles + call center + consulting as one package. Software-only is secondary.

**Planning influence**: Remix gives relationships with planners/consultants. Shapes studies that point to Via for implementation.

**Grant-sourcing**: Helps agencies write grants, pitch councils, sometimes lobby. Consumed significant state funding in California.

**IPO stability**: Public company claims at global scale. Lands with risk-averse buyers.

**Turnkey ROI**: Centralized/offshore dispatch, IC drivers, minimal depots = lower headline cost vs. incumbent operators.

### Where Via falls short

**Paratransit depth** — "Very weak paratransit product." Single wheelchair flag (no per-device rules, boarding times, sequencing). Para + micro riders need two separate accounts. NTD reporting calculation issues. Eligibility imports corrupt/lose data.

**Black-box configuration** — Simple changes (zones, stops, hours) require a ticket to Via, weeks wait, sometimes fees. Agency doesn't own their config.

**Support & reliability** — CARTA: down for nearly a full day (AWS issue), no backup. Rural agencies: "never figuring it out." Offshore dispatch (Philippines) flagged as quality issue.

**Conflict of interest** — Revenue scales with vehicle hours. Integrating NDSPs reduces hours. Structural misalignment with agency cost goals. Operators (Transdev, MV) don't like bidding with Via because Via cannibalizes them.

**Commercial risk** — Post-IPO short-seller reports, class-action investigation on ARR accounting. Still unprofitable despite $434M revenue. Long-term contracts may be exposed.

### Deal examples

**Wins over Via:**
- Alexandria (VA): Via→Spare with WeDriveU. Break clause after unkept promises. UZURV integration sealed it.
- Met Council (MN): Via→Spare microtransit. Better reliability, fixed-route connections, no ridership drop.
- CapMetro: Migrating Pickup microtransit from Via to Spare.
- Brampton Transit: Spare first against Via, RideCo, Pantonium on uninfluenced RFP.

**Losses to Via:**
- Flint MTA: Via in room early shaping vision, supporting grants.
- Rosemead: Spare scored higher and $1M cheaper. Lost on politics.
- Hele-On: Incumbency via MASSTrans framework.
- GoRaleigh, Gainesville, Lake Havasu, Redford: More face time, regional leverage, earlier engagement.

### How to differentiate

1. **Re-frame turnkey as "rigid & conflicted"**: Revenue grows with vehicle hours. NDSPs reduce hours. Misaligned with agency cost goals.
2. **Paratransit complexity**: Met Council, Alexandria, CapMetro — agencies that outgrew Via when para, commingling, NDSPs became critical.
3. **Self-serve config live**: Edit zones, windows, rules in demo. Ask "How does Via change these? How long?"
4. **Reliability & outages**: CARTA story. Rural operator pain. Spare's reliability is more mature.
5. **Neutral orchestrator**: Spare is software, not an operator competing with Transdev/MV. Alexandria proof: WeDriveU chose Spare to avoid Via's conflicts.

## 4. Discovery Questions by Competitor

### RideCo in the picture
- "How much freedom do dispatchers have to override the system or accommodate walk-ups?"
- "How easily can you update eligibility rules and track follow-ups?"
- "Can staff build reports self-serve or do they need vendor support?"
- "How important is integration with your existing fixed-route system?"
- "Have you seen simulations from other vendors? What assumptions did they use?"

### Via in the picture
- "When you want to change zones or service rules, what's the process? How long?"
- "How are para and micro accounts, eligibility, and reporting connected today?"
- "Do you use NDSPs/TNCs? How are they integrated?"
- "How important is it that your software vendor is independent from your operator?"
- "Have you had significant service outages? What was recovery like?"

### Trapeze is the incumbent
- "When an address doesn't show up, what do you do?"
- "How many clicks to book a standard trip?"
- "How does maintenance communicate the down list to dispatch?"
- "How long before a new reservationist is fully productive?"
- "What percentage of calls are ETA checks vs. actual bookings?"
