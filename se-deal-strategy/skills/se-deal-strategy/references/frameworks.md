## 1. MEDDPICC for the Transit SE

**M — Metrics**
- SE role: Help the prospect quantify current pain and expected value. In transit, the metrics that matter are: OTP, PPVH/PPRH, cost per trip, call center volume and handle time, no-shows, complaints, eligibility processing time, and rider satisfaction.
- SE actions: During discovery and on-site, ask for specific numbers. "What's your current OTP?" "How many calls per day does your call center handle?" "How many unscheduled trips are left at the end of each night for schedulers to patch?" Use reference deployment outcomes as benchmarks.

**E — Economic Buyer**
- In B2G, this varies by deal structure. If selling through the agency: the transit director, board, or procurement lead. If through the operator: the VP/SVP of operations or technology at the corporate level (e.g., Stephanie at MV). Sometimes both.
- SE actions: Ask your champion "who needs to sign off?" and "what will they care about most?" Tailor the business case to that audience even if you present to different people.

**D — Decision Criteria**
- Prime SE territory. In transit, criteria often include: FTA/ADA compliance, ability to handle their specific service configurations, proof of scale at comparable agencies, rider experience, integration with existing systems, and total cost of ownership.
- SE actions: Ask early "what are the most important things you're evaluating?" Shape criteria toward Spare's strengths — real-time optimization, unified platform, modern rider experience, proven deployments at comparable scale.

**D — Decision Process**
- B2G adds layers: board approvals, procurement/RFP processes, FTA compliance reviews, sometimes state DOT involvement. Operator deals may be simpler but still require corporate sign-off.
- SE actions: Map the process with your champion. "Walk me through what happens between 'we like this' and contract signature." Understand RFP timelines, board meeting schedules, budget cycles.

**P — Paper Process**
- Government procurement is formal. RFPs, scoring matrices, vendor assessments, sometimes security reviews. The SE often contributes to RFP responses and technical questionnaires.
- SE actions: Ask early about procurement requirements. Offer to proactively provide documentation. Understand the evaluation scoring criteria if possible — this shapes what to emphasize.

**I — Implicate the Pain**
- Go beyond identifying pain to making the prospect feel its full organizational impact. In transit: "Your dispatchers spend 3-5 minutes per brokered trip calling the rider, calling the driver, updating the system. For 300+ brokered trips a day, that's 15-25 hours of dispatcher labor daily on a process that's fully automated in Spare." Use what you see during on-site shadowing to make pain concrete.

**C — Champion**
- In transit, the champion is often the operator GM or an agency program manager with operational credibility. They need to believe Spare won't embarrass them. The SE often has the strongest relationship because the champion values operational domain expertise.
- SE actions: Help them look good internally. Give them materials for meetings you won't attend. Ask "what would make this a win for you personally?" Reference similar roles at similar agencies who championed Spare successfully.

**C — Competition**
- Know whether Trapeze (incumbent), RideCo, Via, or others are in play. See `references/competitive.md`.
- SE actions: Never disparage competitors. Ask questions that highlight Spare's strengths. Create comparison frameworks that play to your advantages. Know where you're weaker and have confident, honest responses ready.

## 2. Stakeholder Mapping — B2G Transit

Transit deals involve stakeholders across multiple organizations. Map who you've engaged:

**Agency Side**
- Transit Director / Executive Director — strategic vision, budget authority, board accountability
- Program Manager (paratransit/MOD) — day-to-day oversight, KPI accountability, often your strongest agency champion
- IT / Technology — integration, security, data, infrastructure concerns
- Procurement — RFP process, compliance, scoring
- Board members — sometimes involved in large technology decisions (political dynamics)

**Operator Side**
- General Manager (site level, e.g., Tony at MV Van Nuys) — operational leader, knows the ground truth, often your strongest operator champion
- AGM / Operations Manager — may be more hands-on with dispatch, scheduling
- Dispatchers, Schedulers, Call Center — the people who use the system daily. Their buy-in (or resistance) matters enormously for change management
- Maintenance Manager — EAM angle, vehicle down-list, DVIR
- IT (if separate from agency IT) — tablets, connectivity, device management
- Corporate leadership (e.g., Stephanie at MV, regional VPs) — signs off on technology decisions, cares about rebid positioning and cross-site standardization

**Categories**
- **Champions**: Want Spare to win, will actively advocate
- **Supporters**: Like the idea but won't push
- **Neutrals**: Haven't formed an opinion or aren't engaged
- **Blockers**: Resistant, skeptical, competing priorities, or comfortable with the status quo

## 3. Discovery Question Bank by Persona

### Operations / Dispatch / Scheduling
- "Walk me through how you build tomorrow's schedule — from when bookings close to when routes are ready."
- "How does the batch process work? How often does it run? What do you do with what's left unscheduled?"
- "When a vehicle breaks down mid-route, what happens? How do you communicate with maintenance?"
- "How do you handle transfer trips coming from other regions?"
- "What's the most frustrating part of your day?"
- "How many workarounds have you built that nobody else knows about?"

### Call Center / Reservations
- "How many clicks does it take to book a standard trip?"
- "When an address doesn't show up in the system, what do you do?"
- "What percentage of calls are just ETA checks vs. actual bookings?"
- "What do you wish the system could do that it can't today?"

### Maintenance
- "How does your team know when a vehicle is due for PMI?"
- "How do you communicate the down list to dispatch? Is that real-time or does it lag?"
- "How does DVIR information get from the driver to your team?"
- "Where do you track work orders?"

### Agency Leadership
- "What's driving the interest in modernizing technology right now?"
- "What does success look like 12 months after a new system goes live?"
- "What are the biggest risks you see in making this transition?"
- "How does this fit into your broader strategic plan or upcoming RFP cycle?"

### Change Management
- "How has your organization handled big technology changes in the past? What went well, what didn't?"
- "Who on your team would be most resistant to change? Who would embrace it?"
- "What does training and onboarding look like for your staff today?"

## 4. On-Site Visit Playbook

### Before
- Align with AE on goals: what to learn, who to meet
- Request agenda with multiple personas: operators, dispatchers, schedulers, call center, maintenance, driver yard
- Ask for an operational shadowing opportunity
- Review everything known about the account
- Prepare tailored questions for each persona

### During
- Observe more than you talk in the first hour
- Watch how people actually work — note the workarounds, the manual processes, the frustrations they've normalized
- Capture details that serve both selling AND implementation: workflow steps, system names, org structure, vehicle configurations, technology stack
- Build rapport with frontline staff — they have the ground truth
- Listen for language the prospect uses and mirror it back in future presentations
- Look for "moments of frustration" — these become your most powerful selling stories

### After
- Send a post-site summary within 24 hours: what you heard, what you observed, proposed next steps
- Write it for the audience above your champion (corporate leadership, agency contacts) because it may be shared
- Debrief with AE — align on strategy based on what you learned
- Update stakeholder map
- Begin tailoring the next demo based on specific scenarios observed
- Note any operational context that would be valuable for the implementation team

## 5. Implementation Handoff — Areas of Awareness

Not a checklist. A reference for the kinds of context the implementation team appreciates receiving. The SE naturally picks up a lot of this through discovery and on-site visits. See `references/operations-discovery.md` for the detailed operational categories.

**What makes a handoff great:**
- A warm intro meeting where the SE bridges the relationship to the project team
- Sharing whatever operational context you've gathered — even partial is better than starting from scratch
- Flagging anything the project team should know that might not be obvious from the contract
- Noting which stakeholders are champions, which are skeptical, and any relationship dynamics

## 6. Deal Health Scorecard

Rate each dimension 1-5. Use the total to gauge deal health and prioritize action:

| Dimension | 1 (Red) | 3 (Yellow) | 5 (Green) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Discovery depth | Surface-level only | Good on some areas, gaps remain | Deep, multi-persona, validated |
| Stakeholder coverage | Single-threaded | 2-3 contacts, missing key layers | Multi-threaded across agency + operator |
| Champion strength | No champion identified | Supporter exists, not yet championing | Strong champion, active and influential |
| Technical win | Major concerns unresolved | Demo'd, some questions open | Proven fit, complexity addressed |
| Business case | No quantified value | Qualitative understanding | Quantified ROI, champion can present it |
| Decision process mapped | Unknown | Partially mapped | Fully mapped with timeline and RFP status |
| Competitive position | Losing or unknown | Competitive, not differentiated | Clearly differentiated on what matters |
| Change management | Not discussed | Acknowledged, not planned | Actively planned, staff getting early exposure |
| Reference alignment | No matching deployment | Partial match | Strong match, prospect aware of reference |
| Partnership positioning | Transactional | Some trust | Trusted partner, prospect seeks guidance |

**Scoring guide:**
- 40-50: Strong deal, focus on close mechanics and handoff
- 30-39: Solid foundation, address specific gaps
- 20-29: Significant gaps, prioritize lowest-scoring areas
- Below 20: Step back — is this deal real? What needs to change to make it viable?

## 7. Deal Strategy Contribution Index (DSCI)

The DSCI measures the SE's strategic impact on a deal, not just participation. Score each dimension 0-3. Total out of 12. Use this for post-deal self-assessment (win or loss) and quarterly deal reviews.

**Implementation**: Review 1-2 deals every quarter. For each: how would the SE rate their input? Did their support shape the outcome? Where could they have provided more strategic influence?

| Dimension | 0 — Reactive | 1 — Supportive | 2 — Influential | 3 — Strategic Owner |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Discovery Depth** | Ran demo without meaningful discovery | Captured stated needs | Identified gaps, risks, stakeholders | Uncovered economic driver, quantified pain, mapped it to a driver for change |
| **Strategic Influence** | No input on win strategy | Validated sales strategy | Suggested differentiation | Helped define & refine win plan |
| **Commercial Impact** | No ROI input | Answered technical objections | Helped shape solution scope | Influenced ROI narrative, architecting solution for customer |
| **Risk Management** | Reacted to objections | Flagged issues late | Identified risks mid-cycle | Proactively qualified out / changed direction |

**Scoring guide:**
- 10-12: Strategic Owner — the SE was a driving force in the deal's outcome
- 7-9: Influential — strong contribution with room to take more ownership
- 4-6: Supportive — participated but mostly followed the AE's lead
- 0-3: Reactive — present but not shaping the deal

**What "Strategic Owner" looks like in transit deals:**
- Discovery Depth 3: You shadowed operations, uncovered pain the prospect had normalized, quantified it (e.g., "30 hours/day of dispatcher labor on brokered trip calls"), and connected it to a business case the champion can present to their board or corporate leadership.
- Strategic Influence 3: You helped the AE build the win plan — which stakeholders to engage, which reference to cite, when to bring in a simulation, how to position against the competitor. The win strategy has your fingerprints on it.
- Commercial Impact 3: You shaped what the prospect is buying and why. The ROI narrative uses numbers you helped gather. The solution scope reflects your understanding of their operation. You didn't just answer "can Spare do X?" — you proposed "here's how we'd architect this for your environment."
- Risk Management 3: You flagged the CityCab API concern before the demo, not after. You identified that the champion might get reassigned and suggested multi-threading. You told the AE "this deal isn't real yet because we don't have the agency engaged" instead of waiting for the loss to say it.

## 8. SE Competency Framework

These are the competency dimensions the SE team is measured against. The skill should coach toward behaviors at the Advanced (4) and Expert (5) levels. Use this as context for what "great" looks like, not as a scoring exercise during deal strategy.

**1. Technical Acumen & Product Knowledge**
- Advanced: Navigates complex technical questions; understands competitive landscape from a technical standpoint.
- Expert: Recognized as a technical authority on the product and ecosystem; shapes product feedback.

**2. Discovery & Business Acumen**
- Advanced: Proactively identifies strategic opportunities; articulates complex ROI models.
- Expert: Trusted advisor to customers on strategic business transformation through product.

**3. Demo Delivery & Technical Presentation**
- Advanced: Creates impactful, customized demos; proactively addresses complex objections and technical concerns.
- Expert: Masterful presenter; consistently leads high-stakes technical conversations and POCs to successful outcomes.

**4. Executive Presence & Communication**
- Advanced: Commanding presence in C-suite and executive briefings. Uses storytelling to make technical content resonate. Quickly reads the room and pivots style in real time.
- Expert: Universally credible across all audience types and seniority levels. Translates technical complexity into business narrative with clarity and impact.

**5. Strategic Alignment & Sales Process Navigation**
- Advanced: Consistently influences deal strategy; proactively identifies technical roadblocks and accelerates sales cycles.
- Expert: Shapes and optimizes sales processes from a technical perspective; critical to closing enterprise deals.

**6. Collaboration & Communication**
- Advanced: Highly skilled at influencing stakeholders; proactively shares insights and fosters strong cross-functional relationships.
- Expert: Key liaison across departments; exceptional at building consensus and driving cross-functional initiatives.

**7. Problem-Solving & Technical Solutioning**
- Advanced: Solves highly complex technical challenges creatively; designs robust and scalable architectures.
- Expert: Innovates technical solutions for unique customer challenges; anticipates future technical needs and risks.

When advising on deal strategy, the skill should nudge the SE toward behaviors that reflect these competencies — not by referencing the framework explicitly, but by suggesting actions that a 4-5 level SE would take. For example: don't just say "do a demo" — suggest a customized demo that addresses the prospect's specific objections and is tailored to the audience in the room (competency 3 + 4). Don't just say "flag the risk" — suggest proactively qualifying out or changing direction based on what the SE observed (competency 5 + 7).
